Research that starts in the classroom,
stays accountable to it, and goes back.

I am an applied linguist and a former classroom teacher. Before pursuing my Ph.D. at Michigan State University as a Fulbright scholar, I spent nine years teaching English in Daegu elementary schools — including a government-designated Multilingual Education Research School. That experience shapes both what I study and how I believe research should work: in genuine, accountable dialogue with the classrooms it claims to be for.

My work asks: how do language teachers sustain themselves emotionally through the demands of diverse, AI-inflected classrooms? How can teachers use GenAI to enhance language learning — especially for multilingual learners whose languages and identities are often invisible in the tools themselves — while building the critical awareness to question those tools? And what does it take to build language education environments where every student’s story counts as legitimate knowledge? My dissertation takes this furthest — using Participatory Design Research, I return to the school where I began teaching in 2012, this time as a co-designer alongside a classroom teacher.

Teaching & Mentoring

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching centers on creating conditions where every learner’s experience, language, and perspective is treated as a resource — not a gap to be filled. I design courses that connect theory to practice, invite critical reflection, and build scholarly confidence that carries students beyond the classroom. For me, teaching and research are not separate: having spent nine years as a classroom teacher before becoming a researcher, I am genuinely committed to classroom-based inquiry that closes the gap between what we know and what teachers can actually use — a commitment that runs through my dissertation, my invited talks on K-12 school research, and my study of how communities of practice bridge research and practice (Foreign Language Annals, 2025).
