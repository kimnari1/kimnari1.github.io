I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Second Language Studies program at Michigan State University, advised by Dr. Peter I. De Costa. I investigate and support the experiences of students and teachers in additional language education — with particular attention to teacher & student emotion and identity, critical digital literacies, and equity and access in multilingual contexts. I also teach current and future language teachers.

Before joining MSU, I worked for ten years as an elementary English teacher in Daegu, South Korea, where I worked closely with young EFL and multilingual immigrant students. That classroom experience shapes both what I study and how I believe research should work: in genuine, accountable dialogue with the classrooms it claims to speak for. It also informs my work as a language teacher educator in the United States, where I teach university courses that prepare pre-service elementary education majors for linguistically diverse classrooms.

I am currently pursuing my Ph.D., supported by a Fulbright Graduate Student Award 🏆 and recognized with the Susan M. Gass Outstanding Research Award 🏆 (2026). I earned my M.A. in Foreign, Second and Multilingual Language Education at The Ohio State University (University Fellowship 🏆; Student of the Year, 2022), and my B.Ed. in English Language Education at Daegu National University of Education.

If you are interested in my work or would like to connect, I’d love to chat. Please feel free to get in touch at kimnari1@msu.edu. You can also find me on ResearchGate, Academia, and Google Scholar.

Research

As a language learner, teacher, and teacher educator, I am curious about how individuals’ emotions and identities shape language learning and teaching in an AI-mediated world that is never ideologically neutral.

My research draws on participatory design-based, ethnographic, narrative, and qualitative methods to investigate these questions. I also collaborate across methodological boundaries, including the use of cognitive tools such as eye-tracking. See below for selected publications and presentations by strands. Paper titles are linked to their respective publications where available.

Teaching & Mentoring

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching centers on creating conditions where every learner’s experience, language, and perspective is treated as a resource — not a gap to be filled. I design courses that connect theory to practice, invite critical reflection, and build scholarly confidence that carries students beyond the classroom. For me, teaching and research are not separate: having spent ten years as a classroom teacher before becoming a researcher, I am genuinely committed to classroom-based inquiry that closes the gap between what we know and what teachers can actually use — a commitment that runs through my dissertation, my invited talks on K-12 school research, and my study of how communities of practice bridge research and practice (Foreign Language Annals, 2025).
